Neuroplasticity and the Growth Mindset

Neuroplasticity

Discuss how understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefits people.

Neuroplasticity is the brain’s capacity to form new neural pathways. Understanding this is important because it means that our intelligence and abilities aren’t fixed traits. When people learn about neuroplasticity, they realise that they can change their brain through challenge and repetition. This mindset helps to build resilience, motivation and a growth mindset.

Discuss how you might engage with the principles of neuroplasticity for your benefit. What are some of the ways to increase your neuroplasticity?

One effective framework to build and maintain neuroplasticity is the AGES principle:

  • Attention: be fully present and focused on what you are learning. Avoid distractions by putting your phone away, and settle in a quiet environment.
  • Generation: Actively associate new concepts with skills and principles you already know. Putting information into context helps see the connection between things and create stronger neural connections.
  • Emotions: Positive emotions strengthen learning. Attaching positive emotions such as excitement, curiosity or joy to new skills, the brain releases dopamine which reinforces neural pathways.
  • Spacing: Regularly revisiting material over time helps build stronger and thicker neural pathways, while also moving the information from short term to long term memory.

Other ways to strengthen and maintain neuroplasticity

  • Keep the brain agile: find activities that challenge you. Getting out of your comfort zone builds new neural pathways.
  • Move your body: Physical activity supports the creation of new neural pathways. Much of the brain is made to move (about 80% of our brain is for motor control), and movement helps regulate mood and stress which is crucial for plasticity.
  • Regulate stress: Chronic stress and high levels of cortisol suppresses neuroplasticity to focus on survival bodily functions. So to protect your brain, aim to practice de-stressing activities such as mindfulness, relaxing activities, breathing exercises and stress coping strategies.
  • Eat a balanced diet: The brain uses 20% of the body’s energy. It is recommended to eat a nutrient-rich diet to support optimal brain function. This means eating lots of fruits, vegetables, whole grains and healthy fat (omega-3 fatty acids found in fish, nuts and seeds), while reducing processed food, red meat and processed sugars to prevent damage.
  • Get good sleep: Your brain consolidates new memories, strengthens connections, and clears toxins during your sleep. Without enough rest, learning and memory formation will be impaired.
  • Avoid alcohol: alcohol is known to wreck havoc into the neurons. It damages brain cells, disrupts neural communication and interferes with memory.

Growth Mindset

Discuss what it is and why it is relevant.

A growth mindset is the belief that abilities and intelligence can be developed over time through dedication, practice and persistence rather than being fixed. The opposite is the Fixed mindset which believes that intelligence and aptitudes are predetermined at birth.

Having a growth mindset fosters a love for learning and growth. People with growth mindsets see challenges as opportunities to improve, instead of a threat. As a result, they tend to stay motivated when facing challenges, and bounce back faster after setbacks.

Some research shows that students with growth mindsets tend to do better academically thanks to their positive self-views and approach to learning, seeing mistakes as part of the learning process, and that effort is the path to mastery.

In this exploration, did anything surprise you? Change for you?

Nothing was particularly surprising to me, as I have already studied the concept of Growth Mindset vs Fixed Mindset in my previous studies as a Health and Fitness Coach. I already actively apply these principles in my career, personal life, and education journey.

I strongly believe that I can do and achieve anything, with the right time, effort and resources. I sometimes get frustrated when I hit roadblocks, but I know that working through challenges will ultimately teach me something valuable. In fact, making mistakes and failing often deepens my learning even more. I live by the idea of “fall twice but get back up thrice.”

How will you integrate a growth mindset into your learning journey?

To integrate Growth mindset principles I will:

  • Embrace challenges by seeing difficult tasks as opportunities instead of fear failure.
  • Persist through obstacles by reminding myself that effort is required to progress and learn.
  • Learn from feedback and criticism as a way for growth and not a personal attack nor a judgment.
  • Reframe setbacks and mistakes as an opportunity for growth.
