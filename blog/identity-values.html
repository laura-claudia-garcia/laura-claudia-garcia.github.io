Identity, values and strengths exploration

Laura Garcia

1 min read 04.08.2025

Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and how you reflect on the decision now.

Two years ago, I was made redundant from a company I had worked at for three years. The entire team was made redundant for the business to transition to a freelancer model to reduce cost in a period of financial instability. This made sense from the company point of view, but the process did not comply with New Zealand’s employment laws, and my colleagues and I were put under extreme stress, we all felt unsupported and betrayed.

A colleague of mine decided to take legal actions against the company and invited all of us to join him. After doing some research on the legal implications and reflecting on my values, I decided not to join him. I felt betrayed and angry from the way we were being let go off, I wanted justice and to support my colleagues, but I also felt deep gratitude and loyalty towards my boss, who had been my mentor and friend. He had taught me everything I knew and kickstarted my career in an incredible manner, and was offering to keep working with him as a freelancer. I knew that preserving that relationship could allow me to continue working with the company. Taking legal actions would have shown support towards my colleagues but it would not have provided me with any positive outcomes, other than potentially some money. This was a moral dilemma because I had to choose between deep values: seeking fairness or maintaining a professional relationship which could potentially support my future. Looking back, I believe I have taken the right decision because I am still very good friends with all of my former colleagues, and I still freelance with my former boss, who deeply trusts me. This experience taught me that ethical decisions involve trade-offs, and that integrity can mean different things depending on what values you choose to prioritise.

Describe how your culture and the people around you have influenced your values and identity.

My father has always taught my sister and I the values of hard work, passion, independence, community and communication. He often worked long hours but taught us that sometimes it was necessary in order to reach your goals, especially when you are passionate about what you do. His deep passion for his work made it easier to work long days. He believed that independence was important so he would encourage us to try to solve things on our own before offering his help. Community and connection were very important to him. Family and friends were always welcome in our home at any time of the day and night. He would organise big family gatherings whenever possible. One of his guiding principles and life work was based on good communication. He believed that communication can solve almost anything, being hard on the problem and easy on the person. My dad’s values greatly shaped the way I approach work and challenges today: I work very hard for what I am passionate about, but I am not afraid to ask for help after trying on my own, I deeply care for friendship and community.

My mother on the other hand showed us the importance of creative expression, emotional expression, gratitude and kindness. She surrounded us with art, took us to the cinema, the theatre, the opera, and encouraged us to explore our creativity through painting, drawing, dancing, and theater classes. She also fostered emotional exploration and self-reflection by creating a safe place to share, be heard and understood. She taught us about kindness through acts of generosity such as volunteering and making sure we would do a number of GA (Good actions) per month. She also encouraged us to practice gratitude by being conscious of what we had. My mother’s values nurtured my sensitivity and the way I approach relationships and personal growth: I see the beauty in everything, I value emotional connection and kindness (and give back to the community through volunteering), and am deeply grateful for the life I have and people in it.

Identify your strengths and how they will support you during your learning journey.

Love of learning and curiosity:
I really enjoy learning and discovering new things, and deepening my understanding of topics I am passionate about. My passion and curiosity keeps me going even when things get hard, and pushes me to exceed expectations by getting really deep into the understanding of a topic.

Kindness and collaboration:
I think that working in a team makes working easier and more enjoyable. Working together allows people to fill each other's gaps and combine their strengths. When working as a team, we can look at challenges through different lenses and come up with solutions you wouldn’t come up with on your own. I really enjoy collaborating with passionate people who push me to be better. I love the proverb that “alone you can go faster , but together you can go further”.

Perseverance and self-discipline:
I don’t give up easily when things get hard. Instead I stay focused and work through obstacles one problem at a time. I’ve developed routines and habits which eliminate the need for motivation and discipline. I love planning in my notebooks, and calendar which allows me to stay on track and adapt when needed in order to achieve short and long term goals.

Evaluate your limitations in terms of your learning and career development. How might these affect your learning journey?

Perfectionism: I often struggle to recognise when something is “good enough” and tend to push for perfection. This allowed me to improve in my career by producing high-quality work and aiming for continuous improvement, but it can also hold me back by making it difficult to efficiently complete tasks and be satisfied with the outcomes. But deadlines usually help with setting time limits.

Humility: I tend to struggle with receiving negative feedback, especially if not expressed in a kind and constructive way. I really value kindness and communication, so when I perceive a feedback as being too harsh, I can quickly become discouraged or hurt. I am working on taking feedback less personally by focusing on what’s the problem that needs to be solved instead of the way it is being said.

Self-doubt: I usually start doubting my worth and capabilities when criticised on my work outcomes. It often leads me to question my abilities to successfully complete what is being asked of me.

Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.

When I was studying at Victoria University, I had a group project to complete for a Video game development paper. We had to design and develop a video game. The team included a few design students (me included) and a few development students. The teams were formed based on votes for the games that were pitched in class. The project quickly became a nightmare due to a lack of communication and structure.

We had no clear assigned roles, and no one respected the members who took the initiative to try to coordinate the project. This meant that tasks were often duplicated, progress was impossible to track, and milestones were missed. I quickly started to feel frustration when I tried to introduce weekly meetings to discuss the project and organise ourselves, but my messages were ignored and meetings were missed. I quickly gave up and focused on the things I could do to meet my course requirements.

Looking back, I’ve learned the value of aligning on a goal, setting roles and communication early on in a project. If this situation were to repeat, I would set up a trello board to break down tasks, assign and track their progress, and set up recurring team catch-ups early on before the project gets momentum.
