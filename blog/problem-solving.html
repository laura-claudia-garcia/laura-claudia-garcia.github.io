Problem Solving Techniques

Laura Garcia

2 min read 31.09.2025

Problem solving is the core skill for any good developer—languages and tools are only secondary to this fundamental skill, and it is often the hardest skill to learn for new developers.

There are a few techniques that can help with the problem-solving process. These include understanding the problem, planning, writing pseudocode, subdividing, and rubber ducking.

Understand

The first step before attempting to solve any problem is to ensure you fully understand it. If you don’t understand a problem, it will be hard to know when you’ve successfully solved it, and you may waste a lot of time on the wrong solutions.

To fully understand a problem, try to reword it in plain English, draw diagrams to visualise it, and explain the problem to a peer or even an inanimate object.

Planning

Once you understand the problem, don’t jump into coding straight away—you first need to plan how you’re going to solve it. Here are some questions to help you plan:

  • Does your program have a user interface? What will it look like? What functionality will the interface have? Sketch this out on paper.
  • What inputs will your program have? Will the user enter data or will you get input from somewhere else?
  • What’s the desired output?
  • Given your inputs, what are the steps necessary to return the desired output?

Once you’ve answered the last question, you can start writing your solution as small steps in pseudocode.

Pseudocode

Pseudocode is writing the logic to get to the solution as small, digestible steps in plain English. It helps you think through the steps needed to reach the desired outcome.

Example: Pseudocode for doing the laundry

// put the laundry in the washing machine
// close the door
// add washing powder in the drawer
// press start

Subdividing

From planning to pseudocode, you’ve now divided your problem into subproblems. Each of the steps in your pseudocode is a subproblem. Pick the smallest and simplest one and start coding. Working this way makes complex problems easier to approach and understand. Your pseudocode might not include every necessary step, but solving subproblems will help you identify any missing pieces.

Rubber ducking

When you hit a roadblock and don’t have anyone to talk it through with, use the rubber ducky method. Rubber ducking involves explaining your coding problem in plain English to an inanimate object. This forces you to walk through your code, explain what you want it to do, and what isn’t working. Talk about the solutions you’ve tried and why they didn’t work, then imagine the questions someone else might ask. This technique helps you articulate the problem differently, step back, and change perspectives—often revealing the start of a solution.

My Experience with problem solving

Blocked by a simple problem

In a recent coding challenge, I had to create a piece of reusable code (called a function) that would return a specific result. Whatever I wrote in the function, the result wasn’t showing and the test was failing. I tried to re-explain the problem in plain english (pseudocode) and then attempted to write the function differently. But to my greatest frustration, the result was still not showing, and the test was still failing. I decided to explain the problem to my partner as well as the steps I took to solve it, but I still could not understand what was wrong. I then searched on google for solutions to similar problems, but it seemed like I was doing the right thing.

Since nothing I tried was working, I decided to ask an AI to point me towards the right direction. That’s when I realised that I had forgotten to add the word return for my function to return the answer. This day, I learnt that if a function doesn’t return anything, it could be because the word return is missing!

Solved a problem elegantly

In the same challenge, I had to write another function to solve a new problem: write a function (piece of reusable code) that takes a sentence, removes the word buzz from it, whether it is capitalized or not, it gives you that cleaned up sentence back. This problem seemed quite tricky. Instead of going straight on the tools, I took some time to analyse the problem to ensure I understood it, then did some pseudocode to break down the problem into small steps, and attempted to first solve the easy parts, ran the test and it passed. I was amazed and super proud that I had managed to solve the entire problem on my own even though at first it seemed quite difficult. It showed me the importance of respecting the process and breaking down difficult problems into tiny achievable steps.

Problem solving techniques confidence

Pseudocode

With the easier exercises, I didn;t quite see the point of pseudo code, but once we started the more difficult challenge, I realised that pseudocode could really help think through the steps needed to resolve an issue without getting too lost. It also helps to make a problem seem less daunting since the small steps should appear quite accessible. I am feeling somewhat confident in my ability to pseudocode. I will definitely get better at thinking through the steps needed once I get more practice and understand the possibilities.

Trying something

When we are first faced with a difficult problem, we may feel overwhelmed or a bit lost. Oftentimes, starting and just trying things will help guide us towards a working solution via trial and error, a lot of console log. Sometimes, a problem that seemed impossible looks a lot more manageable once we start working on it. I feel very confident in my ability to try something as I am a very curious and persisting person.

Rubber ducky method

When getting stuck, explaining the problem and the solutions that were tried and why they failed helps to guide us towards the right path. When I get stuck, I mostly follow this process with my partner which has helped me tremendously already. I am somewhat confident in this ability, but I need to practice the way I explain a problem and what was attempted without letting my frustration takeover.

Reading error messages

When stuck reading errors can point us in the direction of what we need to fix/work on. I am very confident in my ability to check for errors in the console.

Console.logging

Console logging is the ability to pass something to write into the console. It can be extremely useful to check the result of something or to see if something exists. When getting stuck, console logging can help understand what we got wrong/what isn’t working. I am very confident in my ability to do so.

Googling

When different problem solving techniques haven’t worked or when you don’t quite know how to do something, googling the problem or the fact can be a life saver. Specificity is important, so using keywords such as the coding language is key for finding the right answer. I am very confident in my ability to google things when I need more information or help getting unstuck.

Asking your peers or coaches for help

When you’re feeling stuck and a quick google hasn't helped, asking other people for help can be a time saver, instead of spending hours going in circles and getting more and more frustrated. Sometimes, the ego or shyness can get in the way, but asking for help isn’t a sign of vulnerability, it is simply saying “Hey I don’t know this, do you? Could we figure it out together?”. I am very confident in my ability to ask for help when needed.

Improving your process with reflection

After each new challenge, it can be extremely helpful to pause and reflect in order to keep progressing. Some questions you can ask yourself are “what went well?”, “Why did it go well?”, “What do I need to keep doing?”, “What went wrong?”, “Why did it go wrong?”, “What do I need to stop doing?”. I am quite confident in my ability to learn from experiences, but I need to take more time to actually pause and reflect.

Asking for help

I have never really been reluctant to ask for help in an environment that felt safe to do so. The only times I haven;t felt safe asking for help was when I had previously felt judged, rejected or talked down on for not knowing something or understanding something. Otherwise, I usually ask for help when I feel stuck for a certain amount of time before the frustration becomes too high.
